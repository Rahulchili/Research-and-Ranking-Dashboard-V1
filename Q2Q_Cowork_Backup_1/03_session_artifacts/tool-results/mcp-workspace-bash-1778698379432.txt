Key Concepts Rahul Must Understand\n9.1 10-Q\nA 10-Q is the quarterly report filed by a public company with the SEC. Unaudited; contains financial statements and MD&A.\nExtract from 10-Qs: revenue, gross profit, operating income, net income, EPS, cash and equivalents, debt, operating cash flow, capex, segment revenue, guidance discussion.\n9.2 10-K\nAnnual report. It is a sequence:\nPress release / 8-K filed before market open or after close, containing the headline numbers and prepared statements. This is the first informational moment.\nEarnings call, usually 30–90 minutes later, containing prepared remarks and analyst Q&A. This is the second moment.\n10-Q filing, often days to weeks later, containing the full statements and footnotes. This is the third moment.\nFor claim extraction, the relevant date is the call date. For market-reaction analysis (V3+), the relevant date is the press release / 8-K date, since that is when the headline numbers became public. The framework must record all three dates separately and never conflate them.\n9.4 Management Guidance\nFuture-facing statements about expected performance. The XBRL data pulled from SEC filings is GAAP only.\nMapping a non-GAAP guidance claim to a GAAP outcome will systematically score the wrong delivery — sometimes by hundreds of basis points on operating margin. The framework must capture both and use the same basis on both sides of the comparison.\n10. Claim Classification Framework\n11.1 Claim Categories\n11.2 Specificity Score\n11.3 Measurability Score\n11.4 Confidence Tone Score\n11.5 [NEW] Difficulty Score\nA claim that says \"revenue will grow this quarter\" when the company has grown for 20 consecutive quarters is trivial. Management Credibility Score — [REVISED]\n12.1 Simple Version (V1 baseline)\nMCS = Delivered Measurable Claims / Total Measurable Claims\nThis is the published figure for V1 but should never be the only figure. Two known weaknesses:\nVagueness gaming. \"We feel good about the future\" is unfalsifiable; it gets counted as delivered. Companies can score 100% by saying nothing measurable.\nNo difficulty adjustment. Easy claims and hard claims are treated equally.\n12.2 Information-Adjusted MCS — [NEW]\nWeighting each claim's contribution by its specificity addresses the vagueness-gaming problem. Vague-only management ends up with a small \"information footprint\" — many claims, low total weight — making the score visibly low-confidence.\nInformation-Adjusted MCS = Σ(specificity_i × delivery_i) / Σ(specificity_i)\nReport both. A company with MCS = 0.95 and Info-Adjusted MCS = 0.40 is gaming vagueness; a company with MCS = 0.75 and Info-Adjusted MCS = 0.78 is making real, measurable claims and mostly hitting them.\n12.3 Difficulty-Weighted MCS — [NEW]\nDifficulty-Weighted MCS = Σ((1 + 0.25 × difficulty_i) × delivery_i) / Σ(1 + 0.25 × difficulty_i)\nA claim with difficulty +2 contributes 1.5x; a claim with difficulty -2 contributes 0.5x. The 0.25 coefficient is a sensitivity parameter — sweep it across {0.15, 0.25, 0.40} and report the range.\n12.4 Weighted Subscore Version\nMCS_weighted =\n  w_rev × Revenue Delivery\n+ w_mar × Margin Delivery\n+ w_eps × EPS Delivery\n+ w_fcf × FCF Delivery\n+ w_str × Strategic Execution\n[REVISED] The original PRD used weights {0.30, 0.20, 0.20, 0.15, 0.15} without justification. Treat these weights as a sensitivity parameter, not a truth. Report MCS under three weighting schemes:\nDocument which scheme is used for headline rankings and show that conclusions are stable across schemes (or note where they aren't).\n12.5 Delivery Score Formula — [REVISED]\nFor numeric guidance:\nraw_error = |guided - actual| / max(|guided|, ε)        # ε = 1.0 to avoid div-by-zero on small guides\ndelivery_accuracy = max(0, 1 - raw_error)\nbeat_miss_signed = sign(actual - guided)                # +1 beat, -1 miss, 0 in line\nTwo changes from v1:\nFloor at 0. A 200% miss should not produce a negative score that drags the average implausibly low.\nTrack beat/miss separately. A 30% beat and a 30% miss score the same on delivery_accuracy. For credibility analysis, that is appropriate — both indicate the guidance was wrong. But for investment analysis, the asymmetry matters. Keep beat_miss_signed as a separate column.\nExample:\nGuided revenue growth = 10%\nActual revenue growth = 9%\nraw_error = |10 - 9| / 10 = 0.10\ndelivery_accuracy = max(0, 1 - 0.10) = 0.90\nbeat_miss_signed = -1 (modest miss)\n12.6 Directional Claim Scoring\nClaim: \"Margins should improve next quarter.\"\n  margin improves → delivery = 1.0\n  margin flat     → delivery = 0.5\n  margin declines → delivery = 0.0\n12.7 Strategic Claim Scoring\nClaim: \"We expect to launch Product X in Q3.\"\n  on time         → delivery = 1.0\n  late by ≤1 Q    → delivery = 0.5\n  late by >1 Q    → delivery = 0.25\n  not delivered   → delivery = 0.0\n12.8 [NEW] Baseline / Null Model Comparison\nBefore declaring \"MCS = 0.75 is good,\" establish what the score would be under three null models:\nRandom direction. For each directional claim, flip a coin. What's the expected MCS? (Should be ~0.5.)\nAlways say up. Score every claim as \"increase.\" What's the actual MCS? (Often surprisingly high in expansionary periods.)\nAlways restate the trend. Score every claim as \"continuation of the most recent quarter's direction.\" What's the actual MCS?\nA company with MCS = 0.75 in a sample where the \"always say up\" baseline is 0.72 has demonstrated almost no skill. A company with MCS = 0.75 against a baseline of 0.50 has demonstrated real predictive credibility.\nThis is the calibration step that makes the framework meaningful. Without it, MCS is uncalibrated.\n13.
