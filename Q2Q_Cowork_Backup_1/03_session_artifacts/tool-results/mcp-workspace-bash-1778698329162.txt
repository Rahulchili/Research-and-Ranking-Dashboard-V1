Executive Summary\n  [Normal] Rahul is building a local research website that consolidates institutional-style equity research for growth stocks. Each company dashboard contains fundamental analysis, technical analysis, options analysis, valuation comparables, management credibility/MCS, consolidated view, and executive summary. The next product requirement is a portfolio-level ranking engine that compares all analyzed stocks and produces a priority list.\n  [Normal] The ranking system should not be rigid. Key Concepts Rahul Must Understand\n  [Heading 2] 9.1 10-Q\n  [] A 10-Q is the quarterly report filed by a public company with the SEC. Unaudited; contains financial statements and MD&A.\n  [] Extract from 10-Qs: revenue, gross profit, operating income, net income, EPS, cash and equivalents, debt, operating cash flow, capex, segment revenue, guidance discussion.\n  [Heading 2] 9.2 10-K\n  [] Annual report. Audited. It is a sequence:\n  [List Paragraph] Press release / 8-K filed before market open or after close, containing the headline numbers and prepared statements. This is the first informational moment.\n  [List Paragraph] Earnings call, usually 30–90 minutes later, containing prepared remarks and analyst Q&A. This is the second moment.\n  [List Paragraph] 10-Q filing, often days to weeks later, containing the full statements and footnotes. This is the third moment.\n  [] For claim extraction, the relevant date is the call date. For market-reaction analysis (V3+), the relevant date is the press release / 8-K date, since that is when the headline numbers became public. The framework must record all three dates separately and never conflate them.\n  [Heading 2] 9.4 Management Guidance\n  [] Future-facing statements about expected performance. Examples:\n  [List Paragraph] \"We expect revenue to grow in the high teens.\"\n  [List Paragraph] \"Gross margin should improve next quarter.\"\n  [List Paragraph] \"We are increasing investment in data center capacity.\"\n  [List Paragraph] \"We expect free cash flow to improve in the second half.\"\n  [List Paragraph] \"We plan to launch the product in Q3.\"\n  [] The project converts such statements into structured, testable claims.\n  [Heading 2] 9.5 [NEW] GAAP vs. Non-GAAP\n  [] Tech companies almost always guide on non-GAAP measures (excluding stock-based compensation, restructuring, amortization of acquired intangibles) and frequently in constant currency. The XBRL data pulled from SEC filings is GAAP only.\n  [] Mapping a non-GAAP guidance claim to a GAAP outcome will systematically score the wrong delivery — sometimes by hundreds of basis points on operating margin. The framework must capture both and use the same basis on both sides of the comparison.\n  [Heading 1] 10. Management Credibility Score — [REVISED]\n  [Heading 2] 12.1 Simple Version (V1 baseline)\n  [] MCS = Delivered Measurable Claims / Total Measurable Claims\n  [] This is the published figure for V1 but should never be the only figure. Two known weaknesses:\n  [List Paragraph] Vagueness gaming. \"We feel good about the future\" is unfalsifiable; it gets counted as delivered. Companies can score 100% by saying nothing measurable.\n  [List Paragraph] No difficulty adjustment. Easy claims and hard claims are treated equally.\n  [Heading 2] 12.2 Information-Adjusted MCS — [NEW]\n  [] Weighting each claim's contribution by its specificity addresses the vagueness-gaming problem. Vague-only management ends up with a small \"information footprint\" — many claims, low total weight — making the score visibly low-confidence.\n  [] Information-Adjusted MCS = Σ(specificity_i × delivery_i) / Σ(specificity_i)\n  [] Report both. A company with MCS = 0.95 and Info-Adjusted MCS = 0.40 is gaming vagueness; a company with MCS = 0.75 and Info-Adjusted MCS = 0.78 is making real, measurable claims and mostly hitting them.\n  [Heading 2] 12.3 Difficulty-Weighted MCS — [NEW]\n  [] Difficulty-Weighted MCS = Σ((1 + 0.25 × difficulty_i) × delivery_i) / Σ(1 + 0.25 × difficulty_i)\n  [] A claim with difficulty +2 contributes 1.5x; a claim with difficulty -2 contributes 0.5x. The 0.25 coefficient is a sensitivity parameter — sweep it across {0.15, 0.25, 0.40} and report the range.\n  [Heading 2] 12.4 Weighted Subscore Version\n  [] MCS_weighted =\n  []   w_rev × Revenue Delivery\n  [] + w_mar × Margin Delivery\n  [] + w_eps × EPS Delivery\n  [] + w_fcf × FCF Delivery\n  [] + w_str × Strategic Execution\n  [] [REVISED] The original PRD used weights {0.30, 0.20, 0.20, 0.15, 0.15} without justification. Treat these weights as a sensitivity parameter, not a truth. Report MCS under three weighting schemes:\n  [] Document which scheme is used for headline rankings and show that conclusions are stable across schemes (or note where they aren't).\n  [Heading 2] 12.5 Delivery Score Formula — [REVISED]\n  [] For numeric guidance:\n  [] raw_error = |guided - actual| / max(|guided|, ε)        # ε = 1.0 to avoid div-by-zero on small guides\n  [] delivery_accuracy = max(0, 1 - raw_error)\n  [] beat_miss_signed = sign(actual - guided)                # +1 beat, -1 miss, 0 in line\n  [] Two changes from v1:\n  [List Paragraph] Floor at 0. A 200% miss should not produce a negative score that drags the average implausibly low.\n  [List Paragraph] Track beat/miss separately. A 30% beat and a 30% miss score the same on delivery_accuracy. For credibility analysis, that is appropriate — both indicate the guidance was wrong. But for investment analysis, the asymmetry matters. Keep beat_miss_signed as a separate column.\n  [] Example:\n  [] Guided revenue growth = 10%\n  [] Actual revenue growth = 9%\n  [] raw_error = |10 - 9| / 10 = 0.10\n  [] delivery_accuracy = max(0, 1 - 0.10) = 0.90\n  [] beat_miss_signed = -1 (modest miss)\n  [Heading 2] 12.6 Directional Claim Scoring\n  [] Claim: \"Margins should improve next quarter.\"\n  []   margin improves → delivery = 1.0\n  []   margin flat     → delivery = 0.5\n  []   margin declines → delivery = 0.0\n  [Heading 2] 12.7 Strategic Claim Scoring\n  [] Claim: \"We expect to launch Product X in Q3.\"\n  []   on time         → delivery = 1.0\n  []   late by ≤1 Q    → delivery = 0.5\n  []   late by >1 Q    → delivery = 0.25\n  []   not delivered   → delivery = 0.0\n  [Heading 2] 12.8 [NEW] Baseline / Null Model Comparison\n  [] Before declaring \"MCS = 0.75 is good,\" establish what the score would be under three null models:\n  [List Paragraph] Random direction. For each directional claim, flip a coin. What's the expected MCS? (Should be ~0.5.)\n  [List Paragraph] Always say up. Score every claim as \"increase.\" What's the actual MCS? (Often surprisingly high in expansionary periods.)\n  [List Paragraph] Always restate the trend. Score every claim as \"continuation of the most recent quarter's direction.\" What's the actual MCS?\n  [] A company with MCS = 0.75 in a sample where the \"always say up\" baseline is 0.72 has demonstrated almost no skill. A company with MCS = 0.75 against a baseline of 0.50 has demonstrated real predictive credibility.\n  [] This is the calibration step that makes the framework meaningful. Without it, MCS is uncalibrated.\n  [Heading 1] 13.
